The leverage descent: why no position can grow into bad debt

Every perp venue has one nightmare — a position too big for the market to absorb, dumped onto an under-funded backstop. Perpl makes that outcome impossible with a single on-chain rule: as a market's open interest approaches its cap, the maximum leverage on new size descends toward zero. Here's how it behaves — normally, and under a JELLY-style attack. Play with it below.

01

Open interest, and why it has a cap

Open interest (OI) is the total size of all open positions in a market. A cap keeps that total within a sane fraction of the underlying asset — not to guarantee the book can absorb a liquidation, but to keep the market's risk bounded relative to how much of the asset actually exists.

Perpl does not slam a hard door at the cap. Instead, as OI climbs toward the maximum, the contract quietly raises the price of adding more: it reduces the maximum leverage allowed on a new or larger order, forcing more collateral behind size opened near the top. A position opened at the cap is so over-collateralised it essentially cannot go bankrupt from a price move — on liquidation it just returns its own collateral. It never needs the order book, and it never needs the insurance fund.

What it bounds
Solvency, not liquidity — a per-position over-collateralisation limit
Who it looks at
Identity-blind — it keys on the market's total OI, never on who is trading
When it bites
Only opening / increasing near the cap. Closing is always free.
02

The descent, in one curve

One contract function maps a market's forward-looking open interest — the OI the market would have if your order filled — to the maximum leverage you're allowed. Below 90% of the cap, nothing changes. From 90% to 95% leverage falls to 1×. From 95% to the cap it falls to 0.01× (you must post up to 100× the position's value). At or beyond the cap, it's pinned at 0.01×.

Max leverage vs. forward-looking OI. Flat static leverage until 90% of cap, a linear descent to unity (1×) by 95%, then to the 0.01× floor at the cap. The curve is piecewise-linear in leverage; required collateral is its inverse, so it rises gently, then to 1× notional, then to ~100× notional at the cap.

04

The JELLY scenario — the attack the descent defuses

In March 2025 a trader on another venue opened an oversized short in a thin memecoin (~$4M, ~40% of its market cap) at 20×, then pushed the price so the position was force-liquidated into the protocol's market-maker vault. The book was far too thin to absorb it; the vault inherited a deeply underwater position and its unrealised loss peaked around $12–13M. Same pattern as POPCAT and the 2022 Mango exploit: an oversized position in a thin, manipulable market, dumped onto an under-funded backstop.

Drag the attacker's target size below and compare. Without an OI cap and descent, the position grows unbounded on tiny collateral and the backstop eats the gap. With Perpl's descent, the same target runs straight into collapsing leverage — the collateral required explodes, the position is bounded by the cap, and on liquidation it returns its own collateral. The backstop absorbs nothing.

Read it as: the descent doesn't block the attacker — it makes building an oversized position require so much collateral that the position can only be entered if it's already over-collateralised enough to cover itself. The dangerous action (a large, one-sided position near the cap) is exactly the one the mechanism taxes hardest, identity-blind, for everyone.

05

What it means if you trade here

You can always close

The descent only touches orders that open or increase a position. Closing — or reducing — is never penalised, at any OI. If a market is near its cap you are never trapped in a position.

Your liquidation price doesn't move

The descent sets the leverage on new size. The maintenance margin on an existing position — the price at which it liquidates — is fixed when you open it and does not change as the market fills.

Near the cap, opening costs more collateral

If a market is in the top 10% of its cap, a new position simply requires more margin for the same size. The message is "this costs more collateral," never "you're blocked."

It's the same rule for everyone

There's no allowlist and no per-account tier. The descent reads one number — the market's total OI — so it can't be gamed by splitting a position across wallets.

Bound the size to the asset. Descend the leverage before the cap. Keep every position self-insured. No position grows into bad debt. Onchain, by construction.
